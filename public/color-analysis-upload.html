How It Works

Step 1 of 2 — Before You Upload

A few things make all the difference.

Your color analysis is only as good as your photos. These guidelines take two minutes to follow and dramatically improve accuracy. Read each one — there's a reason behind every ask.

Photo Checklist

Natural daylight only

Step near a window or go outside. Indoor bulbs — warm, cool, fluorescent — cast a color temperature onto your skin that can shift your analysis by an entire season family. Overcast daylight is perfect; harsh direct sun is the one exception to avoid.

No makeup, no filter

We're reading your natural skin tone, lip color, and eye color. Foundation changes your undertone reading. Filters, even "natural" ones, alter hue values in ways that are hard to reverse. Come as you are — that's exactly what we need.

Face centered, shoulders in frame

We analyze the color relationship between your face and your natural coloring below the chin — vein color, any chest freckles, and the transition from face to neck all contribute. Cropped headshots lose that context.

Natural hair visible

Your natural hair color is a key data point — it tells us whether your coloring is high contrast, soft, or muted, and whether your warmth is dominant or recessive. Pulled back is fine; dyed is not ideal, but note it in your optional comments.

Plain background, neutral clothing

Busy backgrounds and colorful tops create color bouncing — reflected hues that contaminate your skin tone reading. Wear a white t-shirt or drape a white towel over your shoulders. A plain wall behind you is ideal.

Eyes open, looking toward the light

Eye color is one of three primary signals we use. Pupils contract in bright light and reveal your true iris color — a subtle but meaningful difference for distinguishing cool-tinted eyes from warm-tinted ones.

Your Color Analysis Result

Light Spring

Spring Family · Warm & Clear · Light Value

"The season of new leaves and early blossoms — warm, delicate, and luminous."

Your coloring is defined by warmth and lightness working together. Your best colors reflect the freshness of early spring: clear, warm, and never heavy. When you wear your palette, your skin glows, your eyes brighten, and your natural contrast reads as intentional rather than washed out.

06 — Patterns & Prints

How Prints Work for Your Season

Your coloring is delicate enough that heavy, high-contrast patterns can overwhelm it. The prints that work best for you tend to be organic, light-value, and warm-toned.

Look for prints where the background color is one of your palette colors, and the pattern elements are also within your palette — this keeps the overall effect harmonious rather than clashing.

Watercolor florals Warm ditsy prints Soft paisley Warm animal print Light stripe in palette colors Botanical prints Sun-faded patterns Warm ikat Loose geometric Abstract warm watercolor

Avoid: high-contrast black-and-white prints, cool geometric patterns, dark moody florals, and anything with a black background. These read as a costume on your coloring — the pattern dominates, and you disappear into it.

07 — Hair

The best hair color for a Light Spring is one that looks plausible for your coloring — warm, clear, and light-to-medium in depth. When hair looks natural, the eye goes to the face. When it doesn't, the eye goes to the hair.

Cool or very dark hair color can create a stark contrast against your light, warm face. If you're going darker, stay in the warm brown family and keep it at least two shades lighter than you think you want to go.

08 — Makeup

A Palette That Enhances, Not Transforms

Light Spring makeup should look like you, only more so. The goal is warmth, freshness, and clarity — not drama. When your makeup is in your palette, the overall look appears effortless even when it isn't.

Foundation Warm or neutral-warm undertone. Look for descriptors like "peach," "golden," "warm beige." Avoid anything described as "pink," "cool," or "rosy."
Blush Warm peach, apricot, and soft coral work beautifully. Avoid cool pinks and berry tones. A light dusting of warm peach can double as an eyeshadow.
Lip Warm coral, peachy-pink, and clear warm rose. Your lips likely have natural warmth — enhance it rather than replacing it with a cool or dark shade.
Eye Shadow Peach, warm taupe, warm gold, coral-toned champagne, and soft warm brown. Avoid grey, charcoal, and cool-toned purples near the eye — they flatten your features.
Liner Warm brown is your most flattering liner. Black liner can work for evening but should be smudged to soften it. Avoid stark black pencil for daytime.
Bronzer Yes — use a warm, golden-toned bronzer rather than a terracotta or cool-toned one. You tan beautifully and bronzer plays up that quality.
Highlighter Gold or champagne — never silver or icy pink. A warm dewy finish suits you more than a blinding metallic.

09 — Accessories

Metal, Stone, and Material

Metal

Yellow gold, rose gold, warm brass, and burnished copper. Silver can work when it has warmth — white gold or platinum tend to look cold.

Gemstones

Warm topaz, citrine, amber, peridot, warm turquoise, and coral. Diamonds work when set in warm metals. Sapphires and amethysts can pull cool.

Bags & Shoes

Warm tan, camel, cognac, warm taupe, and ivory. Avoid grey, true navy, and cool-toned black leathers — they visually disconnect from your coloring.

Eyewear

Warm tortoise, honey blonde acetate, warm gold frames, and light warm brown. Avoid stark black frames and cool grey metals near your face.

Scarves & Wraps

Any palette color works beautifully as a scarf near the face — this is your best opportunity to experiment with bolder palette choices without committing to a full outfit.

Fabric Texture

Soft, natural textures suit you — linen, cotton, light wool, silk. Heavy structured fabrics in dark colors can overwhelm your lightness. Stay in light-to-medium weights.

10 — Neighboring Seasons

Your Color Family

Light Spring shares characteristics with two neighboring seasons. Understanding the overlap helps when you encounter colors that feel close but don't quite land.

True Spring

Sister Season — same family

True Spring shares your warmth but can carry slightly more saturation and a touch more depth. If a color looks right but a shade too vivid for you, it likely belongs to True Spring. You can borrow from True Spring as long as you keep the value light.

Light Summer

Neighboring Season — shared value

Light Summer shares your lightness but leans slightly cooler and softer. Colors at the overlap point — powder blue, soft pink, light lavender — can work for both. If something feels almost right but faintly cool, it may be a Light Summer color wandering into your territory.

If you ever feel uncertain between Light Spring and True Spring, the tell is clarity: True Spring can handle more vivid, saturated color. If a color is vivid and feels too strong, step toward Light Spring. If a color feels warm and right but slightly muted, consider that it may be crossing toward Light Summer — and can still work for you if the warmth is there.

11 — Shopping With Your Season

How to Use This in Real Life

A color analysis is most valuable when it changes how you make decisions, not when it creates rules that paralyze you. Here's how to put this to work practically.

The 3-second test: When you pick something up, ask — is this warm or cool? Is it light or dark? Clear or muted? If the answer matches your coloring (warm, light, clear), it's worth trying. If it fights all three, put it back.

Near the face: This is where your palette matters most. A navy blazer at the waist is far less impactful than a navy turtleneck. Prioritize your palette for necklines, scarves, earrings, and frames.

Occasion exceptions: A black dress for a formal event is fine. Your coloring won't collapse. The goal is to stock your everyday wardrobe with colors that make getting dressed easier and better — not to create anxiety over every purchase.

When in doubt, test with a scarf: Drape a color near your face in a mirror, ideally in natural light. If your skin looks clearer and your eyes brighter, it's in your zone. If you look tired, the color is competing.
