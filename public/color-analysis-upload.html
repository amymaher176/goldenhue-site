How It Works

Step 1 of 2 — Before You Upload

A few things make all the difference.

Your color analysis is only as good as your photos. These guidelines take two minutes to follow and dramatically improve accuracy. Read each one — there's a reason behind every ask.

Photo Checklist

Natural daylight only

Step near a window or go outside. Indoor bulbs cast a color temperature onto your skin that can shift your analysis by an entire season family. Overcast daylight is perfect; harsh direct sun is the one exception to avoid.

No makeup, no filter

We're reading your natural skin tone, lip color, and eye color. Foundation changes your undertone reading. Filters, even "natural" ones, alter hue values in ways that are hard to reverse.

Face centered, shoulders in frame

We analyze the color relationship between your face and your natural coloring below the chin — vein color, any chest freckles, and the face-to-neck transition all contribute.

Natural hair visible

Your natural hair color is a key data point — it tells us whether your coloring is high contrast, soft, or muted. Pulled back is fine; dyed is not ideal, but note it in your optional comments.

Plain background, neutral clothing

Busy backgrounds and colorful tops create color bouncing — reflected hues that contaminate your skin tone reading. Wear a white t-shirt or drape a white towel over your shoulders.

Eyes open, looking toward the light

Eye color is one of three primary signals we use. Pupils contract in bright light and reveal your true iris color — a subtle but meaningful difference for distinguishing cool-tinted from warm-tinted eyes.

Tips for a better photo

  • Stand near a window in natural daylight (not direct sun)
  • No makeup, filters, or heavy retouching
  • Face centered and fully visible, shoulders in frame
  • Plain white or neutral background behind you
  • Eyes open and looking toward the light source
